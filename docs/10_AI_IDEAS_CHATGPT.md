I cannot honestly prove that nobody has proposed any of these. What I can say is that I pressure-tested each one against adjacent categories where AI is already active—symptom checkers, grief tools, mediation, caregiving, end-of-life planning, wildlife monitoring, and conservation—and I did not find a clear product or mainstream public proposal that matches these exact applications. Adjacent products do exist in those nearby spaces, which is why I cut several weaker ideas that felt too close.



10 ideas

1\) Identity Firewall



2-sentence version: You create a “constitution of yourself” while calm and healthy: what you care about, what your red flags look like, and what decisions you should never make in a bad state. Later, if you start acting unlike yourself during burnout, addiction, grief, mania, or coercion, the AI does not obey instantly—it slows you down, shows you your own prior values, and routes you to trusted humans.



What it does

A personal AI that is not optimized to please current-you; it is optimized to protect long-term-you from temporary-you.



Why nobody built it

Most AI products are designed to maximize compliance and convenience. A system that sometimes says “no, this is not the version of you that should decide this” is commercially awkward and ethically scary.



What makes it hard

It must distinguish genuine growth from dangerous drift. It also needs careful consent design, escalation thresholds, and a way to avoid becoming paternalistic.



Why it matters

A lot of life damage happens in temporary states. We have legal and medical advance directives, but we do not have a practical identity advance directive for everyday decisions.



Devil’s advocate

This could become manipulative or lock people into an outdated self-image.

Why it survives: make it user-authored, revocable, transparent, and limited to high-impact domains like money, messaging, self-harm-adjacent spirals, and relationship detonations.



2\) Foster Continuity Engine



2-sentence version: When a child moves between foster homes, hospitals, schools, or relatives, the AI carries forward the human continuity that institutions lose: bedtime rituals, food aversions, phrases that calm them, fears, nicknames, grief triggers, trusted stories, and what “home” has meant so far. It turns fragmented records into a living “how to not erase this child again” guide for the next adult.



What it does

A portable continuity layer for children whose lives are repeatedly reset by systems.



Why nobody built it

Child welfare data is fragmented, sensitive, and legally messy. Also, most systems are built for compliance, not for preserving personhood.



What makes it hard

Extreme privacy requirements, messy source material, trauma-informed UX, and the need to avoid freezing a child into labels.



Why it matters

The damage in foster systems is not only bad decisions; it is repeated identity rupture. This directly attacks that.



Devil’s advocate

This could create a surveillance dossier around a child.

Why it survives: the design has to be explicitly child-protective, minimal, human-audited, and centered on continuity and comfort—not prediction or risk scoring.



3\) Symptom Metaphor Translator



2-sentence version: Many people cannot describe what hurts in medical language, so the AI interviews them using metaphors, comparisons, body maps, voice notes, and “is it more like pressure, electricity, glass, or heat?” It converts vague suffering into a clean clinician-ready description with uncertainty labels and a timeline.



What it does

It translates subjective, messy experience into something a doctor can actually use.



Why nobody built it

Healthcare AI has focused on diagnosis and triage, not on the pre-diagnostic translation problem. But a huge amount of medical failure starts before diagnosis, when the patient simply cannot explain themselves.



What makes it hard

You need careful medical prompting, multilingual nuance, strong uncertainty handling, and zero hallucinated certainty.



Why it matters

People are often dismissed because their symptoms sound disorganized. This would especially help chronic illness, pain, neurodivergent patients, migrants, and anyone under stress.



Devil’s advocate

It could generate polished nonsense that misleads clinicians.

Why it survives: make it explicitly non-diagnostic, source-linked to the patient’s own words, and designed as a translation aid rather than an answer machine. Existing AI symptom tools focus on triage, which is a different job.



4\) Grief Objects Curator



2-sentence version: After someone dies, families drown in objects they cannot emotionally process. This AI helps them sort belongings by story, emotional weight, family meaning, conflict risk, and “keep / digitize / gift / release,” without pretending to resurrect the dead person.



What it does

It is not a dead-person chatbot. It is a grief and inheritance triage system for the physical world.



Why nobody built it

The AI grief market chased “talk to your loved one again,” because that is flashy. The harder, less glamorous problem is the mountain of things sitting in houses for years.



What makes it hard

Family conflicts, sentimental asymmetry, and the fact that value is narrative, not monetary.



Why it matters

Unprocessed belongings can freeze grief, trigger sibling wars, and leave homes untouched for years.



Devil’s advocate

This could feel cold or push people to discard too fast.

Why it survives: the AI should slow people down, not speed them up, and preserve stories before making disposal suggestions. Current grief-AI attention has mostly centered on memory avatars and memorial interaction, not this physical-belongings layer.



5\) Blackout Mutual-Aid Allocator



2-sentence version: During a power outage, the AI turns one neighborhood into a temporary operating system: who has freezer space, oxygen backup, insulin cooling, baby formula, power tools, phone charging, safe beds, and car capacity. Instead of chaos and group-chat noise, it matches needs to nearby resources minute by minute.



What it does

A disaster-time allocator for civilian survival, using existing phones, forms, maps, and messaging.



Why nobody built it

Disaster tech usually aims upward at utilities and governments, not sideways at neighbors. Mutual aid exists, but mostly as spreadsheets, maps, and social trust—not real-time allocation logic.



What makes it hard

Verification, fraud resistance, low-connectivity operation, liability, and the fact that crises destroy clean data.



Why it matters

Hours matter in outages. This is one of the few AI ideas that could save lives without requiring new hardware.



Devil’s advocate

People may not trust it enough in an emergency.

Why it survives: start with churches, apartment blocks, and rural communities that already self-organize; AI just reduces coordination friction. I found mutual-aid resources and AI-for-grid prevention, but not a clear consumer-facing local allocator built for civilian outage triage.



6\) Species Coexistence Scheduler



2-sentence version: Instead of only fencing animals out or warning drivers, this AI schedules human activity around wildlife rhythms: when to dim lights, delay drone flights, reroute joggers, alter delivery windows, or slow traffic based on local species movement. It treats cities and farms as negotiations between species, not just human territory with animal exceptions.



What it does

A planning layer that converts wildlife detection into human behavior changes.



Why nobody built it

Conservation AI mostly stops at detection and monitoring. Very little of it turns those insights into daily operational schedules for ordinary human systems.



What makes it hard

You need reliable local data, behavior-change compliance, and interfaces that planners, parks, farms, and neighborhoods can actually use.



Why it matters

A lot of biodiversity loss comes from constant small human disruptions rather than one dramatic event.



Devil’s advocate

Humans will ignore wildlife-friendly schedules.

Why it survives: target places where incentives align—parks, campuses, ports, farms, resorts, road authorities, protected corridors. Today’s visible AI work is strong on tracking and detection, but not on routine coexistence scheduling.



7\) Taste Memory Reconstructor



2-sentence version: You remember your grandmother’s soup, but there is no recipe—only fragments like “it smelled a little sweet at first, then peppery, and the broth went cloudy when she stirred it.” The AI interviews memory, culture, era, ingredient availability, family geography, and cooking habits to reconstruct the most likely versions and help you cook your way back to a lost person or place.



What it does

A memory-forensics engine for food.



Why nobody built it

Recipe AI mostly starts with ingredients or dietary goals. It does not start from grief, migration, dementia, or half-remembered sensory traces.



What makes it hard

Human taste memory is unreliable, family recipes mutate, and the AI has to reason probabilistically across culinary tradition and personal variation.



Why it matters

Food is identity storage. For migrants, bereaved families, and aging people, recovering one dish can recover continuity.



Devil’s advocate

Maybe it just invents fake nostalgia.

Why it survives: that is why it should generate ranked hypotheses and iterative tasting loops, not present one fantasy answer as truth. I found AI recipe generators and family-recipe preservation tools, but not a clear product centered on reconstructing lost recipes from sensory memory alone.



8\) Future Interface Clone



2-sentence version: While you are healthy, the AI quietly learns the exact visual, linguistic, and interaction patterns your brain finds easiest. Decades later, if aging, stroke, cognitive decline, or fatigue make normal apps unusable, your devices reshape themselves into your own old cognitive style instead of forcing you to adapt.



What it does

A personal accessibility time capsule.



Why nobody built it

Accessibility is usually designed as generalized presets. This idea requires treating usability as something deeply individual and worth preserving over time.



What makes it hard

Long-term data continuity, privacy, OS integration, and separating temporary preference from durable cognitive fit.



Why it matters

Aging often feels like being exiled from interfaces designed for younger cognition. This would preserve agency instead of adding one more layer of helplessness.



Devil’s advocate

People’s needs change too much for an old model to stay relevant.

Why it survives: it should adapt continuously, but with a stable baseline anchored in the person’s earlier strengths.



9\) Argument Divergence Mapper



2-sentence version: After a serious conflict, each person separately tells the AI what happened. It does not “solve” the argument; it identifies the exact moments where shared reality split—different assumptions, different definitions, different remembered promises, different emotional timelines.



What it does

A post-conflict forensic tool for couples, families, teams, and co-founders.



Why nobody built it

Most communication AI writes better messages. Very little tries to map the structural point of divergence between two incompatible realities.



What makes it hard

It must avoid false neutrality, manipulation, and overconfidence while handling deeply biased inputs.



Why it matters

Many relationships do not end because of one event; they end because two people never locate the moment they stopped meaning the same thing.



Devil’s advocate

This is just automated mediation with nicer framing.

Why it survives: mediation usually pushes toward settlement; this focuses first on epistemic reconstruction—where the tracks split. AI mediation exists nearby, which is exactly why this would need tight scoping to stay distinct.



10\) Conditional Legacy Release



2-sentence version: Instead of leaving one static bundle after death, you leave an AI-organized library of stories, explanations, documents, and messages that unlock only under meaningful future conditions: when your child becomes a parent, when siblings finally ask about the family rupture, when a grandchild visits your hometown, when someone is old enough for the truth. It is a moral timing system for legacy.



What it does

A memory and truth-release engine based on developmental context, not just date of death.



Why nobody built it

End-of-life tech has focused on wills, account access, memorial pages, and chatbot clones. Timing wisdom is much harder than storing files.



What makes it hard

Condition design, governance, consent, family conflict, and preventing manipulative “messages from beyond the grave.”



Why it matters

A lot of family truth is not absent; it is badly timed. This makes legacy more humane and less blunt.



Devil’s advocate

This could become emotional remote-control from the dead.

Why it survives: strict guardrails—no live persuasion, no coercive asks, only explanation, story, and practical guidance. Current end-of-life AI products I found are mostly about planning assistance or digital-presence continuation, not conditional truth release.



Rank by impact on humanity

1\. Foster Continuity Engine



Because it intervenes where institutions repeatedly fracture identity during childhood.



2\. Identity Firewall



Because enormous human damage comes from decisions made in destabilized states.



3\. Symptom Metaphor Translator



Because millions of bad medical encounters begin with failed translation, not failed medicine.



4\. Blackout Mutual-Aid Allocator



Because it can convert existing communities into survival systems during outages and disasters.



5\. Species Coexistence Scheduler



Because it scales human-wildlife peace from isolated projects to daily operations.



6\. Future Interface Clone



Because aging populations need agency-preserving accessibility, not generic simplification.



7\. Grief Objects Curator



Because unresolved physical inheritance quietly traps families for years.



8\. Conditional Legacy Release



Because timing is one of the least-designed parts of intergenerational truth.



9\. Taste Memory Reconstructor



Because food is a powerful bridge for migration, grief, dementia, and family continuity.



10\. Argument Divergence Mapper



Because it could save some relationships, but misuse risk is high and impact is more localized.



Top 3 product concepts

1\) AnchorSelf



Pitch: “Your values, defending you from your worst temporary self.”



First 1,000 users:

People with bipolar disorder who want guardrails they control, people in addiction recovery, high-stakes professionals under burnout, and couples who want pre-agreed crisis decision limits.



2\) CarryHome



Pitch: “A continuity layer for children whose lives keep being reset.”



First 1,000 users:

Pilot through foster agencies, pediatric oncology units, emergency kinship-care networks, and child advocates handling repeated placements or long hospital stays.



3\) PainTongue



Pitch: “Turns hard-to-explain suffering into language doctors can work with.”



First 1,000 users:

Chronic pain patients, autoimmune patients, women with long diagnostic delays, migrants navigating a second language, and primary-care clinics that deal with vague or complex complaints.



The three that most strongly pass the holy shit, why does that not exist test are Foster Continuity Engine, Identity Firewall, and Symptom Metaphor Translator.
